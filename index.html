Exercise 1 – Standing Broad Jump

A study examined physical fitness levels of 10-year-old children who regularly participate in sports. The 900 participants were divided evenly between three groups according to their training level:

  • Low: training less than 1 hour per week
  • Moderate: training between 1 and 5 hours per week
  • High: training more than 5 hours per week

Each participant performed the standing broad jump fitness test. The mean results are shown in the graph.

Source: © International Baccalaureate Organization 2018

a.i [1]
Identify the training level and sport for the group that has the highest mean score on the standing broad jump fitness test.
a.ii [2]
Calculate the difference of mean standing broad jump fitness test score between moderate and high training levels for the group stated in 1(a)(i). Give your answer in cm.
a.iii [2]
Using the data, deduce the effect of high level of participation in sport on performance in the standing broad jump test.
a.iii [3]
A two-tailed, unpaired t-test was conducted. Results: low vs moderate: p > 0.05; moderate vs high: p < 0.05; low vs high: p < 0.01. Comment on the meaning of the results from the t-test.

  • Condition 1: Shoulder pads (with and without)
  • Condition 2: Shoulder (dominant vs non-dominant)
  • Condition 3: Setting (laboratory vs rugby field)

Table 1 shows the mean maximum impact force (N) and standard deviation for Conditions 1 and 2.

Table 2 shows the mean maximum impact force for trials in the laboratory and on the field (Condition 3).

A recent study tested the effect on impact force (N) of adding a soft-covering cap to a helmet when dropped from a set height. The graph shows results for helmets A, B, and C.

Source: Usman et al., Journal of Science and Medicine in Sport, 2011

a.i [1]
Identify which shoulder has the greatest mean impact force.
a.ii [2]

Exercise 3 – Field Hockey Practice Groups

A study investigated the effect of practice on improvement of four field hockey skills. Participants engaged in pre-test and post-test competitions before and after a six-week training programme. Participants were randomly allocated to one of three practice groups:

  • Fixed
  • Variable
  • Game-based

Results for successful performance of each skill during competitions are shown in the table (* p < 0.05).

a.i [1]
State the mean percentage for successful passing by the fixed practice group in the pre-test competition.
a.ii [1]
Identify the practice group and skill with the highest mean percentage of successful post-test performances.
a.iii [2]
Calculate the difference in mean percentage between successful pre-test and post-test game-based trapping.
a.iv [4]
Using the data, deduce the effect of each practice group on each skill.

Exercise 4 – Plyometric Exercise & Sprint Speed

A study investigated the effect of plyometric exercise on sprint speed. Mean speed was measured during a 20 m sprint as a baseline and then in five further sprints. Between sprints, participants did one of:

  • Plyometric: three sets of alternate leg bounds
  • Control: continuous walking

The graph shows mean sprint speed and positive standard deviation for both conditions.

A paired t-test comparing mean sprint speed at 4 minutes with baseline: Plyometric: p < 0.05; Control: p > 0.05.

a [1]
Identify the time and condition with the highest mean sprint speed.
b [2]
Calculate the difference in mean sprint speed (m s⁻¹) between baseline and at 8 minutes for the plyometric condition.
c [2]
Using the data, discuss the hypothesis that plyometric exercise can improve sprint performance.
d [2]
Explain the reason for using a control condition in this study design.
